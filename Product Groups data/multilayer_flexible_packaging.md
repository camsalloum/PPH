## Chapter 1: Introduction

**Authors:** John R. Wagner Jr. (Crescent Associates) and Scott B. Marks (E.I. du Pont)

### 1.1 Materials: A Historical Perspective

- **Cellophane** was the first common clear film, invented by Jacques E. Brandenberger (Swiss textile engineer) in **1908** — inspired when a restaurant customer spilled wine on a tablecloth. He developed a machine for manufacturing transparent regenerated cellulose sheets. E.I. du Pont obtained the rights and improved the process, adding:
  - Nitrocellulose moisture barrier coating → better stiffness retention
  - Heat-sealable coating → first readily sealable transparent packaging film
  - PVdC (polyvinylidene chloride) coating → added oxygen and moisture barrier → original non-metal barrier film for food packaging
- **Polyethylene (PE)**:
  - First synthesized accidentally in **1898** by Hans von Pechmann while heating diazomethane.
  - First industrially practical PE: Eric Fawcett and Reginald Gibson at ICI Northwich, UK, **1933** (accident via trace oxygen contamination). Michael Perrin developed it into reproducible high-pressure synthesis; LDPE commercial production started **1939**.
  - Phillips catalyst (chromium trioxide) discovered **1951** by Robert Banks and J. Paul Hogan → HDPE.
  - Karl Ziegler developed a titanium halide / organoaluminum catalyst in **1953** → Ziegler-Natta.
  - HDPE production near failed commercially in 1957 — rescued when the **hula hoop** toy became a US fad.
  - Metallocene (single-site) catalysts discovered **1976** by Kaminsky and Sinn.
  - Post-metallocene catalysts (e.g., salicylaldimine complexes of Group 4 metals) developed by Fujita/Mitsui → higher activity.
- **Polypropylene (PP)**:
  - First polymerized by Dr. Karl Rehn at Hoechst AG, **1951**; rediscovered by Giulio Natta, **March 11, 1954**.
  - Biaxially oriented PP (BOPP) introduced in **1960s** — first blown film process (DuPont, Iowa, USA), then tenter frame process made it more economical → replaced cellophane.
- **PET (polyethylene terephthalate)**: Patented **1941** by John Rex Whinfield and James Tennant Dickson (Calico Printer's Association, Manchester) following on Wallace Carothers' (DuPont) research. First polyester fiber "Terylene" (ICI), then DuPont's "Dacron."
- **Polystyrene**: Discovered **1839** by Eduard Simon. Commercialized by I.G. Farben ~1931. Expanded polystyrene (EPS) foam developed by Koppers Company, Pittsburgh, **1959**.

- The flexible packaging film market has grown substantially post-WWII through the 21st century.
- US flexible plastic packaging films 2002–2007 (million lb):

- Global markets feature varying penetration — some countries with minimal, some with average, and some with high usage of flexible plastic packaging.

### 1.3 Processes, Materials, Needs

**Coextrusion** is used in:
- Blown film
- Cast film (< 10 mils / < 250 μm)
- Cast sheet (> 10 mils / > 250 μm)
- Blow molding
- Tubing and sheathing
- Extrusion coating and laminating
- Profile

**Why coextrude?**
- Bring desired properties of differing materials into one structure
- Reduce emissions from solvents used in adhesive lamination
- Process multiple materials in one pass → save process steps, cost, time

**Scientific considerations in coextrusion:**
1. **Thermal** — processes are non-isothermal
2. **Fluid** — non-Newtonian viscoelastic fluids with non-linear temperature-dependent viscosities and interfacial boundaries
3. **Mechanical** — metal containing fluid is subjected to internal pressure forces

**Typical coextruded structure layers:**
- Bulk layer
- Barrier layer
- Sealant layer
- Adhesive/tie layer(s) (for incompatible layers)
- Reclaim layer

**Common bulk layer resins:**
- PE: HDPE, LLDPE, LDPE, VLDPE
- PP: HoPP, CoPP, Ter-PP
- Acrylates: EMA, EBA, EEA, PMMA, EMMA
- EVA (low/medium/high VA%)
- PS: HIPS, GPPS

**Criteria for bulk layer:**
- Cost, adhesion, flexibility, softness, stiffness
- Melt index (MW and MWD), density/crystallinity, molecular branching, comonomer content, melt point

### 1.4 Materials and Barrier

Barrier types and resins:

| Polymer | Barrier Properties |
|---|---|
| Nylon/Polyamide (PA) | Oxygen, aroma, oil barrier |
| EVOH | Oxygen, flavor/aroma barrier |
| PET | Moisture, some flavor/aroma, some chemical |
| PVdC | Moisture, oxygen, flavor, aroma, some chemical |
| HDPE | Moisture barrier |
| PP | Moisture barrier |
| Ionomer | Oil, some chemical barrier |

**Nylon (PA) grades for flexible packaging:**
- Nylon 6: MP = 220°C (430°F) — most common in flexible packaging coextrusions
- Nylon 66: MP = 250°C (480°F)
- Nylon 6/66: MP = 210°C (410°F)
- Amorphous Nylon: used in blends
- Nylon 11/12: MP = 180°C (360°F)

**EVOH characteristics:**
- Good O₂ barrier when dry; almost always run in coextrusion
- Needs tie layers (except to Nylon); moisture sensitive; thermal sensitivity → needs short, smooth flow paths
- Good optical properties; high stiffness; flex crack sensitivity
- EVOH history: DuPont original patents → Monsanto → Japanese corporation → now major suppliers are Kuraray (Eval®), Evalca (subsidiary of Kuraray in USA), Nippon-Gohsei (Soarnol®)

**PET extrusion grades:**
- APET (amorphous), CPET (crystalline), PETG, PBT, PPT, PTT, PEN
- Also PLA, PGA, PHA (biopolymers)

**Other barrier resins:** PAN, SAN, AN-MA (Barex®), ABS, LCP, COC (Topas® or Apel®), PUR (extrudable), SBC (K-Resin®)

### 1.5 Materials and Sealing

**Seal methods:** Thermal (most common), radiofrequency, high frequency, ultrasonic, pressure sensitive.

**Sealant layer considerations:**
- Hot tack strength, heat seal strength, seal initiation temperature
- Sealing speed, coefficient of friction
- Ability to seal through contaminants, chemical resistance, economics

**Common sealant resins (high to commodity performance):**
- Ionomers of acid copolymers
- Acid copolymers (EAA or EMAA)
- mVLDPE (very low density metallocene)
- EVA or EMA blends with LLDPE
- CoPP/TerPP, EVA, EMA, LLDPE, mLLDPE
- LDPE or PP

### 1.6 Tie Resins

**Purpose:** Chemical and/or mechanical bonding between incompatible polymer layers; adhesive strength depends on tie resin formulation and coextrusion process parameters.

**Base resins:** LDPE, LLDPE, HDPE, EVA, PP, acrylate copolymers, acid copolymers

**Common modifiers:**
- Maleic anhydride (grafted)
- Olefinic tougheners, tackifiers, rubbers

**Suppliers:** DuPont (Bynel®), Mitsui (Admer®), Equistar, Mitsubishi, Arkema, Rohm & Haas, Dow, DSM

**Key considerations for tie resin selection:**
- Match rheology to adjacent materials (no interfacial instability)
- Temperature limitations (EVA: max 235°C/455°F)
- Minimum temperature needed for chemical/mechanical bonding
- Process time (time between die exit and solidification)

**Complete generic resin nomenclature** (when brand/grade unknown):
- mLMDPE, mLLDPE, mVLDPE, BEnBACO, EiBAMAA, EVA-gMAh, LLDPE-gMAh, Peel Seal (peelable sealant), TPS (thermoplastic starch)

## Chapter 2: PE Processes

**Author:** Thomas I. Butler (Blown Film Technology, LLC)

### 2.1 Introduction

PE types covered: LDPE, LLDPE, m-LLDPE, HDPE, VLDPE, Enhanced PE (EPE)

**Basic raw material:** ethylene (C₂H₄, MW = 28), a colorless gas with slightly sweet smell, obtained by cracking (thermal decomposition) ethane at high temperatures (~800°C) in a steam furnace. Ethane is from natural gas, oil, or naphtha.

**Key PE history:**
- LDPE first discovered by ICI 1932; first commercial plant 1939
- 1953: Ziegler-Natta catalyst → HDPE and LLDPE at low temperature/pressure
- 1973: Supported Z-N catalyst → 1,000-fold efficiency improvement (1,000,000 lb polymer/lb Ti)
- 1978: LLDPE introduced using Z-N catalyst + alpha-olefin comonomer
- 1993: Single-site/metallocene catalyst technology introduced

### 2.2 Melt Index (MI)

- **Definition:** Grams per 10 minutes pushed out of a die of prescribed dimensions (ASTM D-1238) under a specified load.
- **Conditions for PE:** 2.16 kg load at 190°C; die D = 2.095 mm, L = 8 mm (Condition E)
- **Units:** g/10 min = dg/min
- **Blown film PE range:** 0.01–30 dg/min
- **Relationship to molecular weight:** Low MI → high MW; high MI → low MW
- Wall shear rate: `γ̇ ≈ (1838/ρ) × MI` where ρ = melt density in kg/m³ (≈766 for PE at 190°C) → `γ̇ ≈ 2.4 × MI`
- MI for extrusion PE grades rarely > 12; injection molding: MI typically 5–50

### 2.3 Density and Crystallinity

- **Short-chain branching (SCB)** and **long-chain branching (LCB)** control resin density
- As octene comonomer level increases → density decreases
- Higher density → higher crystallinity → higher modulus

**Crystallinity equations:**
- Weight % crystallinity: `Xw = [(ρ - ρa)/( ρc - ρa)] × (ρc/ρ) × 100`
  where ρa = amorphous density = 0.853 g/cc, ρc = crystalline density = 1.000 g/cc
- Volume % crystallinity: `Xv = [(1/ρ - 1/ρa)/(1/ρc - 1/ρa)] × 100`

**PE density ranges:**
- HDPE: 0.940–0.965 g/cc
- MDPE: 0.925–0.940 g/cc
- LDPE: 0.915–0.925 g/cc
- LLDPE: 0.915–0.940 g/cc
- VLDPE: 0.900–0.915 g/cc
- ULDPE: 0.900–0.915 g/cc

### 2.4 Molecular Weight Distribution (MWD)

- **Gel Permeation Chromatography (GPC)** measures MWD
- Mn = number-averaged MW; Mw = weight-averaged MW
- **Polydispersity (PDI) = Mw/Mn**
  - LLDPE from single reactor Z-N: PDI = 3.5–4.0
  - Bimodal polymers (multiple reactors): PDI up to 20
  - Single-site (metallocene) catalysts: PDI = 2–3
- Wider MWD → better processability; narrower MWD → better film strength, lower extractables, better optics

**Polymer chain structure types:**
1. Linear (HDPE, PP, PS, PC, PA)
2. Short-chain branched (LLDPE)
3. Short- and long-chain branched (LDPE)
4. SCB with controlled LCB (patented SSC systems)
5. Cross-linked

### 2.5 Free Radical Processes (LDPE)

**Autoclave reactor:**
- Stirred cylindrical, ~6.1 m long × 0.38–0.91 m dia
- Pressure: 15,000–30,000 psi (100–200 MPa)
- Free radical initiator: peroxide or oxygen
- Conversion: ~22% per reactor pass
- Production: 150–450 M lb/year
- Produces root-like branching distribution

**Tube reactor:**
- Long pipe ~1.25 km long, 2.5–7.6 cm dia
- Pressure: 20,000–50,000 psi (140–350 MPa)
- Initiator injected at multiple points along tube
- Conversion: ~35% per reactor pass
- Production: 200–500 M lb/year
- Produces comb-like branching distribution

**LDPE chain mechanisms:**
- Chain propagation: free radical + ethylene → LCB
- Back-biting: creates butyl SCB (4-carbon branch)

**LDPE Homopolymer:** Both LCB and SCB incorporated. SCB creates crystal defects → smaller lamella → lower density.

**PE Copolymers (via free radical process):**
- EVA (ethylene vinyl acetate) — largest volume; VA randomly distributed; polar functionality → low seal initiation temperatures; higher VA = higher density, lower crystallinity
- EAA (ethylene acrylic acid) — polar + H-bonding → good adhesive to metals/polyamides
- EMAA (ethylene methacrylic acid) — similar to EAA
- Ionomers — EMAA neutralized with Na or Zn → ionic cross-links → high melt strength, excellent heat seal/hot tack

### 2.6 Transition Metal Catalyst Processes (HDPE, LLDPE)

**Processes:** Gas phase, Solution, Loop slurry, Slurry

**Gas phase process:**
- PE precipitates as solid powder in a fluid bed
- Temperature < 115°C (must stay solid)
- Pressure: ~350 psi (2.4 MPa)
- Comonomers: butene, hexene, or octene
- Can produce: Mw from very high to moderately low; density 0.91–0.96; MWD 3.2–30
- Catalysts: Z-N, chrome-based, metallocene

**Solution process:**
- Polymer in liquid state, temperature > 150°C, pressure ~500 psi
- Comonomer: typically octene (can be other alpha-olefins)
- Produces: moderate to very low Mw; density 0.89–0.96; MWD 3.4–30
- Catalysts: Z-N, metallocene

**Loop slurry process:**
- Two phases: liquid (ethylene + diluent) + solid (polymer)
- Temperature < 90°C, pressure < 300 psi (2 MPa)
- Produces: ultra high to moderate Mw; density 0.91–0.96; MWD 3.5–30

### 2.7 Ziegler-Natta (Z-N) Catalyst Evolution

- Early (1964–1973): ~1,000 lb polymer/lb Ti
- Supported catalyst (1973–present): 1,000,000 lb polymer/lb Ti (MgCl₂ + Ti(OR)₄ + Al(C₂H₅)Cl₂)
- Multiple active sites (A, B, C) → different MW and branching distributions
- Heterogeneous branching distribution → broad DSC melting curve
- Site A: low MW / high branching; Site B: medium MW / medium branching; Site C: high MW / low branching

### 2.8 Chrome-Based Catalysts

- Used in slurry and gas phase processes
- Produce very high MW polymers
- Applications: blow molding, pipe, thermoforming, high stalk blown film
- May incorporate some LCB → improved melt strength
- Broad MWD; bimodal distributions with multiple reactors

### 2.9 Single-Site Catalysts (SSC/Metallocene)

- Metallocene: metal atom (e.g., zirconium) stabilized with cyclopentadienyl rings
- Uniform reactive site → very narrow MWD (Mw/Mn = 2.0–2.5) and narrow CD (comonomer distribution)
- Benefits: increased film strength, lower extractables, better optical properties
- Disadvantage: narrow MWD makes processing more difficult (higher amps, temps, pressure)
- SSC polymers with LCB (e.g., INSITE process): higher I₁₀/I₂ → better shear sensitivity/processability

**Comonomer effect on film properties (at 1.0 MI, 0.919–0.920 g/cc):**

| Comonomer | Dart Impact (g/mil) | MD Elmendorf Tear (g/mil) | MD Tensile (MPa) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Butene (C4) | 100 | 100 | 33.1 |
| Hexene (C6) | 200 | 300 | 36.5 |
| Octene (C8) | 335 | 400 | 46.9 |

Higher alpha-olefin → longer SCB → improved film properties.

### 2.10 Enhanced Polyethylenes (EPE)

- Molecular architecture modification to optimize both processability and solid-state properties
- Controlled MWD and CD (comonomer distribution)
- Design goals: optimize tie molecule concentration and lamellar thickness simultaneously
- Tie molecules: amorphous molecules with ends embedded in lamellae → critical for toughness and tear properties
- Optimum density ~0.915 g/cc for maximum tie molecule effectiveness (tie concentration vs. lamella restraint trade-off)
- EPE can be produced using multiple reactors, mixed catalyst systems, or blending
- Properties improved over LLDPE/m-LLDPE: impact, tear, sealability, optical, or modulus

## Chapter 3: Polypropylene

**Author:** Allison Calhoun, PhD (Whitman College)

### 3.1 Overview

PP is used in multilayer film for its strength, toughness, high melting point, and processability with appropriate additives.

**Basic structure:** Methyl group on every other methylene unit → stereocenters → different tacticities.

**Tacticities:**
- **Isotactic PP (iPP):** Methyl groups all on same side → high crystallinity, high MP (160–180°C), higher density and strength. >95% isotactic (by pentad analysis via NMR). 40–70% crystallinity.
- **Syndiotactic PP (sPP):** Methyl groups alternating; lower MP (~138°C vs. 155–160°C), lower density (0.89 vs. 0.93 g/cm³); crystallizes slowly.
- **Atactic PP (aPP):** Random methyl placement → low crystallinity, soft, tacky; high vapor/gas permeability. Used in adhesives and roofing.

### 3.2 Polymerization Methods

**Ziegler-Natta catalyzed PP:**
- Catalyst: TiCl₃ + trialkyl aluminum
- Produces predominantly isotactic PP with PDI ≈ 3.5 (broad MWD)
- High MW achievable (~4 × 10⁶ g/mol)
- Four polymerization systems: slurry, liquid propylene, solution, gas phase

  - **Slurry:** Chain growth in hydrocarbon solvent; insoluble fraction (isotactic) separated by centrifugation; soluble fraction (atactic) removed by boiling off solvent.
  - **Liquid propylene:** Monomer = polymerization solvent; prevents runaway reactions via vaporization of excess heat; isotactic and atactic both in insoluble phase, separated with organic solvent.
  - **Solution:** Monomer + catalyst + solvent at 175–250°C; vacuum drying removes solvent; both isotactic and atactic remain.
  - **Gas phase:** Gaseous monomer through fluidized bed of granules + supported catalyst; unreacted monomer collected and recycled.

**Metallocene catalyzed PP:**
- Single active site → tighter tacticity control, narrower MWD (Mw/Mn ≈ 2.0), reduced catalyst residues
- Can produce both isotactic and syndiotactic PP with controlled properties
- Slightly lower MP than best Z-N products due to catalyst-specific defects
- Can be used in solution (non-polar diluent) or gas phase (supported on inert substrate)

### 3.3 Crystalline Forms of Isotactic PP

**Alpha (α) crystallinity:**
- Most thermodynamically stable; forms on cooling below crystallization temperature
- Helical polymer chain conformation → monoclinic unit cell → crystallites → lamellae
- Cross-hatching: secondary lamellae grow perpendicularly off primary lamellae → spherulites
- Spherulites can be size of visible light wavelength → opaque/translucent film; high haze
- Nucleating agents can reduce spherulite size below light wavelength → more transparent film

**Beta (β) crystallinity:**
- Forms on rapid cooling below melting point; lower density and lower MP than alpha
- Reverts to alpha upon heating or strain

**Gamma (γ) crystallinity:**
- Rarely from standard processing; forms by shearing alpha crystalline polymer → two-directional growth

**Smectic/Mesomorphic crystallinity:**
- Rapid quenching prevents large crystalline domain formation
- Properties between alpha crystalline and atactic PP
- Transparent film; reverts to alpha upon heating

### 3.4 Processing Challenges

**Degradation mechanisms:**
- Thermo-oxidative: oxygen + high temp → autocatalytic reaction → double bonds, chain scissions, low MW volatiles → yellow discoloration, holes or thin spots in film
- MW reduction → reduced viscosity → ineffective mixing and pumping → changed film properties
- **Stability ranking:** PP < HDPE ≈ LLDPE < LDPE

**Processing requirements:**
- Confirm stabilizer effectiveness under actual processing conditions (residence time, shear, temperature)
- Extra care with recycled material (depleted stabilizer)

**Melt strength challenges:**
- PP generally low melt strength → unstable bubble in blown film (sagging, dancing bubble)
- Solution: Long-chain branched PP grades → increased entanglements → greater cohesion → higher melt strength

**Opacity:**
- Orientation breaks up spherulitic structure → transparent film
- Orientation also improves: tensile properties, impact strength, reduces gas permeability

**PP in multilayer films:**
- Can be major structural layer or minor functional layer
- Coextrusion joining done inside die (blown/cast) or at hot nip
- PP copolymer (ethylene-propylene or ethylene-butylene-propylene) used as skin layer → lower melting point → good heat seal

## Chapter 4: Additives to Design and Improve the Performance of Multilayer Flexible Packaging

**Authors:** Klaus Keck-Antoine, Els Lievens, Joachim Bayer, John Mara, Duk-Suk Jung, Sung-Leal Jung (Songwon Industrial and partners)

### 4.1 Introduction and Classification

**Two principal approaches to modify polymer properties:**
1. Structural modification of the polymer
2. Incorporation of additives

**Additive categories:**
- **Stabilizers:** Maintain original molecular architecture (mechanical, optical, organoleptic) during storage, conversion, service life. Subcategories: antioxidants, UV stabilizers.
- **Modifiers:** Improve or alter performance. Subcategories: optical brighteners, slip agents, antiblock agents, antistats.
- **Fillers:** Different surface-to-volume ratio; added at higher amounts; improve physical properties or cost-dilute. Examples: talc, chalk.

### 4.2 Additive Handling, Addition, and Dosing

**Addition points in polymer lifecycle (Fig. 4-1 reference):**
- (A) Monomer stage
- (B) Reactor
- (C–E) Post-reactor operations (flash, catalyst deactivation, drying)
- (F) Storage silo
- (G) Extrusion (basic grades)
- (H) Compounding (fully formulated grades)
- (I) Conversion/film manufacturing
- (K) Recycling

**For multilayer films:** Not every additive needs to be in every layer. UV absorbers may only go in skin layers. Migration between layers must be considered (partition coefficient based on solubility in each phase).

**Physical forms:**
- Masterbatch (20–50% active ingredients in a compatible carrier)
- One-pack (100% active ingredient)

**Layer-specific additive incorporation (generic 3-layer film):**

| Additive | Skin Layer I | Core Layer II | Skin Layer III |
|---|---|---|---|
| Antioxidants & heat stabilizers | ● | ● | ● |
| UV absorber | ● | ○ | ○ |
| HA(L)S | ● | ○ | ○ |
| Optical brighteners | ● | | |
| Migrating slip | ● | ○ | ● |
| Non-migrating slip | ● | | ● |
| Antiblock | ● | | ● |
| Antistats | ● | | ● |
| Neutralizers | ● | ● | ● |

● = recommended; ○ = limited/conditional

**Concentration effect (saturation curve):**
- Phase A: Below critical concentration — no measurable effect
- Phase B: Effect increases with concentration
- Phase C: Effect levels off (saturation)
- Phase D: No further improvement; secondary effects (plate-out, blooming, cost without benefit)

**Optimal concentration:** Phases B or C with safety margin for industrial dosing inaccuracy.

### 4.3 Antioxidants and Other Stabilizers

**Effect:** Protect organic substrates from thermal and thermo-oxidative degradation during storage, processing, service life in absence of UV light.

**Stability ranking:** PP < HDPE ≈ LLDPE < LDPE

PP and some PE: degrade exclusively via MW reduction; other PE grades: MW reduction + cross-linking.

**Principle:**
- Without stabilizer: properties deteriorate immediately on processing
- Ideal stabilizer: retains properties throughout processing
- Real stabilizer: delays onset and slows degradation rate

**Antioxidant chemistry for polyolefins:**
- Synergistic combination: **phenolic antioxidant (AO) + phosphite (P)**
- Phenolic AO: process protection (also extends service life)
- Phosphite: process protection (prevents color during processing)

**PP recommendations:**

| Case | Effect | Additive | Load Level (ppm) |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | General purpose | AO-1 + P-1 | 750 + 750 |
| B | Severe processing | AO-1 + P-1 | 750 + 1500 |
| C | Reduced color | AO-3 + P-1 | 500 + 1000 |
| D | Low water-carry-over | AO-4 + P-1 | 1000 + 500 |
| E | Severe processing (top-up) | AO-1 + P-3 | 1000 + 1000 |

**LLDPE recommendations:**

4. Hydroperoxide decomposition

- Surface effect; defensive tool to mask unavoidable discoloration

- Limited compatibility, limited photo-stability
- Only after other approaches fail to reduce discoloration
- Example: FWA-1 (2,5-thiophenediylbis(5-tert-butyl-1,3-benzoxazole), Uvtex OB by Ciba)
- Load levels: PP/PE: 10 ppm; PET: 200 ppm; PVC: 75 ppm; PA: 200 ppm

### 4.6 Slip Additives

**Function:** Reduce coefficient of friction (COF) between film surfaces → improve handling, conversion, packaging line speeds.

**Mechanism (migrating slip):**
- Phase A: Homogeneously dispersed; low surface concentration; high COF
- Phase B: Migration to surface due to incompatibility; increasing concentration; decreasing COF
- Phase C: Equilibrium; continuous layer at surface; minimum COF

**Migration rate and minimum COF depend on:**
- Substrate crystallinity
- Slip additive (in)compatibility (aliphatic chain length)
- Slip agent concentration
- Temperature

**Chemistry:** Fatty acid amides from natural sources (vegetable/animal)

| Amide Type | Migration Speed | Low COF Achievable | Thermal Stability | Other |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Erucamide (E) — primary | ++ | +++ | ++ | AB (limited) |
| Oleamide (O) — primary | +++ | ++ | + | |
| Ethylene bis-oleamide (EBO) — secondary bisamide | + | + | +++ | AB in polar polyolefin copolymers; multilayer |
| Stearyl erucamide (SE) — secondary | + | + | +++ | AB; multilayer |
| Oleyl palmitamide (OP) — secondary | + | + | +++ | Multilayer |

For multilayer films: longer chain, slower migrating slip agents preferred (EBO, SE, OP).

**Starting point formulations:**
- PP: erucamide 900 ppm; PE: erucamide 1000 ppm; PET/PVC: 2000 ppm

**Migrating slip in layers:** Mainly skin layers; can also go in core.

### 4.8 Antistats

**Function:** Dissipate electrostatic charges → reduce dust attraction, improve processing.

- Cationic, anionic, amphoteric (less common in flexible packaging)

**Incorporation:** Mainly compounding (G, H); often added separate from solid additives.

## Chapter 5: Rheology of Molten Polymers

**Authors:** John Vlachopoulos (McMaster University / Polydynamics Inc.) and David Strutt (Baycomp Ltd.)

### 5.1 Introduction

- **Rheology** (coined 1929 by E.C. Bingham): study of deformation and flow of matter
- **Society of Rheology** formed 1929; motto: *"panta rei"* (all things flow, Heracleitus 6th century BC)
- **Continuum hypothesis:** Material (solid or fluid) is an infinitely divisible substance
- **No-slip condition:** Fluid at solid surface takes on velocity of solid surface (Vfluid = Vwall)
- Flow properties depend on: molecular weight and molecular architecture

### 5.2 Viscosity and Melt Flow Index

**Viscosity definition:**
```
η = (F/A) / (U/h) = shear stress (σ) / shear rate (γ̇)
```

**SI units:** Pa·s (1 Pa·s = 10 poise)

**Typical values:**
- Water: 10⁻³ Pa·s
- Polymer melts during processing: 10² to 10⁵ Pa·s

**Non-Newtonian (shear-thinning) behavior:** Polymers love shear — as shear rate increases, viscosity decreases due to molecular alignments and disentanglements.

**Power law model:**
```
η = m · γ̇^(n-1)
```
where m = consistency index, n = power-law exponent (0 < n < 1 for shear-thinning).
- n = 1: Newtonian
- For various PE grades: 0.3 < n < 0.6
- PC: n ≈ 0.8; rubber compounds: n ≈ 0.2

**Better models for full range:**
- Carreau-Yasuda: `η = η₀[1 + (λγ̇)ᵃ]^((n-1)/a)`
- Cross model: `η = η₀ / [1 + (λγ̇)^(1-n)]`

**Melt Index (MI) → power law parameters:**
```
n = [log(HL) - log(LL)] / [log(HLMI) - log(MI)]
m = 8982 × (LL) / [(1838 × MI/ρ)^n]
```

**Intrinsic viscosity (IV):**
```
[η] = K × M_v^a    (Mark-Houwink equation)
```
For bottle-grade PET resins: IV = 0.70–0.85 dl/g; higher IV → higher MW → higher melt viscosity.

**K-value for PVC:** Measure of molecular weight from IV in cyclohexanone; range 35–80. Low K = easy to process, inferior properties; high K = difficult to process, outstanding properties.

**Shear rate ranges in processing:**
- Single-screw extruder screw channel: up to 200 s⁻¹
- Die lip/exit: up to 1000 s⁻¹
- Injection molding: up to 10,000 s⁻¹

**Wall slip:** Occurs at ~0.1 MPa shear stress for PE. Measured by Mooney method (apparent shear rate vs. 1/R for different capillary radii).

**Temperature sensitivity:**
```
η = η_ref × exp(-β × ΔT)
```
β ranges 0.01–0.1°C⁻¹. For common polyolefins: β ≈ 0.015. Temperature increase of 10°C → ~14% viscosity decrease. HDPE: β = 0.01; LDPE: β = 0.03.

**Zero-shear viscosity vs. MW:** `η₀ ≈ const × M_w^3.4`

### 5.3 Extensional Viscosity and Melt Strength

**Trouton ratio (Newtonian fluids):**
```
η_e / η = 3
```

**Extensional viscosity in practice:**
- Polymer melts can exhibit **strain hardening** (η increases with stretch time/strain)
- LDPE: strain hardening in extension (high extensional viscosity) → more stable blown film bubble
- LLDPE: linear chains slide easily in extension (lower extensional viscosity) → less bubble stability
- Most PP: very low melt strength → unstable blown film bubble
- **Melt strength:** Engineering measure of resistance to extension; max force to break extruded strand

**Cogswell's method** for extensional viscosity from entrance pressure drop (ΔPe):
```
η_e = 9(n+1)² × (ΔPe)² / (32 × η × γ̇²)    at ε̇ = 4ηγ̇² / (3(n+1)ΔPe)
```

### 5.4 Normal Stress Differences and Extrudate Swell

**First Normal Stress Difference:** `N₁ = σ_xx - σ_yy`

**Second Normal Stress Difference:** `N₂ = σ_yy - σ_zz` (negative, ~20% of N₁)

N₁ very sensitive to high MW tail; broad MWD → high N₁.

**Extrudate swell (die swell):** Diameter of extrudate > die diameter due to stress relaxation at exit. Swell ratio can reach 400%+ under extreme conditions. Increases with:
- Molecular weight distribution broadening (for linear polymers)
- Shorter die length
- Higher melt temperature gradient

**Stress ratio:** `SR = N₁w / (2σw)` can reach 10 or more at onset of melt fracture.

### 5.5 Stress Relaxation and Dynamic Measurements

**Storage modulus G'** (elastic component) and **Loss modulus G"** (viscous component):
- G' > G" → more elastic; G" > G' → more viscous
- Crossover point (G' = G") correlates to PDI for PP

**Cox-Merz rule:** `η(γ̇) = |η*(ω)|` — allows frequency sweep to substitute for difficult high shear rate viscosity measurements; valid for most polymers.

**Relaxation time (longest):**
- From steady shear: `λ = N₁/(2ηγ̇²)` at γ̇→0
- From dynamic: `λ = G'/(G"ω)` at ω→0
- Higher λ → more elastic polymer

**Constitutive equations:**
- Newtonian: σ = ηγ̇
- Power law: σ = mγ̇ⁿ
- Maxwell model: σ + λσ̇ = ηγ̇ (predicts stress relaxation, not extensional behavior)
- Advanced: K-BKZ integral model (24+ parameters); Doi-Edwards; Pom-Pom model (H-polymer structure, 32+ parameters)

### 5.6 Sharkskin, Melt Fracture, Die Lip Build-up

**Sharkskin (surface mattness):**
- Onset at critical wall shear stress ~0.14 MPa (21 psi) for most polymers
- Repetitive wavy/ridged pattern perpendicular to flow direction
- Mechanism: stick-slip near die exit
- Remedies: fluorocarbon processing aids (deposit on die surface → continuous slip), boron nitride, heated die lips, small exit angle (flaring), special die lip coatings

**Gross melt fracture:**
- Highly distorted extrudate + pressure fluctuations at higher shear rates
- Proposed mechanisms: entry vortex instability, elastic instability in die land, stick-slip, other polymer-metal interactions

**Die lip build-up (die drool):**
- Gradual accumulation of initially liquid deposit at die exit → solidifies → obstructs flow
- Rich in low MW polymer fractions, waxes, additives
- Remedies: repair plating/surface imperfections, remove moisture, lower extrudate temperature, add stabilizer, fluorocarbon processing aids, small exit angle

### 5.7 Rheological Problems in Coextrusion

**Layer non-uniformity:**
- Less viscous polymer migrates to high shear regions (wall) → encapsulation
- Contributing factors: polymer wall adhesion, viscoelastic characteristics (N₂)
- Can occur in annular or slit dies

**Feedblock profiling:**
- Contouring feedblock flow passages for regions of high/low volumetric throughput
- Counteracts natural encapsulation tendency due to viscosity differences

**Zig-zag (die-land) interfacial instability:**
- Most common interfacial instability; chevrons pointing in flow direction
- Critical interfacial shear stress: 40–80 kPa (~¼ to ½ of critical wall shear stress for sharkskin)
- Appears even with same material in both layers
- Remedies: decrease output rate, increase skin layer thickness, decrease skin layer viscosity (raise temperature or use lower viscosity polymer), increase die gap

**Wave pattern instability:**
- Parabolas spanning width oriented in flow direction
- Occurs when fast-moving stream merges with much slower stream in feedblock

### 5.8 Rheometers

| Instrument | Purpose | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Melt indexers | Quality control; rough shear-thinning estimate | 1 point (2.16 kg) + HLMI |
| Capillary viscometers | Viscosity curve; Rabinowitsch/Bagley corrections; extensional via Cogswell; melt strength | 1–3000 s⁻¹ |
| Rotational (cone-and-plate/parallel plate) | G', G", dynamic viscosity; first normal stress difference; full viscosity range via time-temperature superposition | 10⁻² to 5 s⁻¹ (steady); up to 1000 rad/s (dynamic) |
| Torque rheometers | Quality control (complex geometry; not accurate) | — |

## Chapter 6: Coextrusion Equipment for Multilayer Flat Films and Sheets

**Author:** Eldridge Mount III (EMMOUNT Technologies)

### 6.1 Overview

**Purpose of a multilayer film/sheet die:** Produce polymer film with uniform thickness suitable for subsequent film processes; for multilayer systems, uniform layer dimensions.

**General configuration of coextrusion system:**
1. Individual extruders, filters, melt pumps
2. Melt pipes (deliver molten polymers)
3. **Adapter** (collects and routes melts)
4. **Feedblock** (shapes and combines melts pre-die)
5. **Die** (spreads, thins, distributes melt uniformly)

**Two principal approaches:**
1. Feedblock coextrusion
2. Multi-cavity die coextrusion
3. Combined feedblock + multicavity dies

### 6.2 The Adapter

- Bridge between extrusion system and coextrusion system
- Acts as extension of melt pipes + routes streams to various inlet locations
- **Strategic importance:** Should be sized to allow future layer additions WITHOUT replacing existing melt pipes/extruder relocations
- **Fixed geometry adapters:** No cross-contamination; changing layer positions requires adapter change or resin feed changes
- **Interchangeable flow path adapters:** Routing flexibility via interchangeable body segments or removable plugs; minimal cross-contamination risk

### 6.3 Feedblock Coextrusion

**Principle:** Multiple melt streams combined in a relatively small feedblock before entering a single-cavity die. All layers combined before distribution.

**Advantages:**
- Lower cost (single cavity die)
- Better inter-layer adhesion
- Suitable for many-layer structures where die cavity cost would be prohibitive

**Disadvantages:**
- Some materials difficult to coextrude this way (viscosity mismatches → encapsulation)
- Less control over individual layer distribution vs. multicavity

**Feedblock design elements:**
- Flow-diverting vanes or selector plugs
- Profiling (contouring flow passages) to compensate for viscosity-driven layer migration
- Critical to match viscosities or intentionally mismatch for stable layers

### 6.4 Multicavity Die Coextrusion

**Principle:** Each polymer has its own cavity in the die; individual layers spread and distributed before combining.

**Advantages:**
- Best control over individual layer uniformity
- Each layer can be separately temperature controlled
- Better for materials with very different processing conditions (e.g., high-temperature EVOH adjacent to low-temperature sealant)

**Disadvantages:**
- More complex and expensive die
- Purging and changeovers more difficult

### 6.5 Flat Die Design (Coat-Hanger Die)

**Die purpose:** Distribute uniform flow across full die width.

**Coat-hanger manifold:**
- Central manifold with diverging geometry (like coat hanger shape)
- Controls residence time and pressure uniformity across width
- Designed to give uniform flow rate across full width for a given polymer at given conditions

**Die components:**
- Manifold: primary distribution
- Preland (transition): secondary distribution/adjustment
- Land (die lips): final shaping and determining final film gauge

**Layer uniformity considerations:**
- Width and thickness uniformity of each layer
- Layer flatness across width
- Avoidance of weld lines or streaks

**Flat die for thick sheets (thermoforming):** Similar design but higher throughput; must handle higher pressure from viscous polymers.

### 6.6 Case Studies in Equipment Configuration

**Case 1: Simple 3-layer film (e.g., PE/tie/PA)**
- Minimum: single feedblock with 3 extruders + single-cavity die
- More stable: feedblock with profiling or 3-cavity die

**Case 2: 7-layer barrier film (food packaging)**
- LLDPE/tie/PA/EVOH/PA/tie/LLDPE (or similar)
- Options: 3-extruder + feedblock (splitting layers) or 7-cavity die or feedblock + multicavity combination
- Layer splits allow fewer extruders while maintaining structural symmetry (A/B/C/D/C/B/A from 4 extruders)

**Case 3: 3–7 layer thermoforming sheet with PA, EVOH, PVDC**
- Structures: LLDPE/tie/nylon/tie/nylon/tie/LLDPE or PP/tie/nylon/EVOH/nylon/EVA/LDPE etc.
- Relatively thick layers → layer uniformity less critical; multicavity or feedblock+multicavity combination works
- Must accommodate recycled scrap layer(s)

**Key summary points:**
- Size adapter to allow future changes without melt pipe replacement
- Choose technology based on: number of layers, viscosity differences, temperature requirements, future product flexibility
- Specify accurate polymer rheology data to die manufacturer
- Evaluate supplier's knowledge of coextrusion science — if not interested in polymer rheology, likely just building rather than truly designing

## Chapter 7: Multilayer Blown (Tubular) Film Dies

**Author:** John Perdikoulias (Compuplast International Inc.)

### 7.1 Historical Development

**Pre-1970s:** Two-layer coextrusion dies common; barrier materials introduced in late 1970s–early 1980s.

**Barrier materials** (PA, EVOH, PVDC) have much smaller processing windows than polyolefins → more sensitive to poor die design and poor operating practice.

**Conventional coextrusion dies (1970s–1980s):**
- Rotating/oscillating block systems (e.g., Barmag provided 150+ dies by mid-1980s)
- Concentric nested cylinders → poor purge characteristics, long changeover times
- Rotating die systems for gauge randomization → maintenance problems, polymer hang-up

**Key improvement ~late 1980s:** Widespread introduction of **oscillating haul-off** → removed gauge randomization requirement from die → manufacturers focused on channel design for lower residence times.

### 7.2 Conventional Cylindrical Spiral Mandrel Distribution

- Each layer: conventional single-layer spiral mandrel distribution system
- Spiral grooves control material leakage → layering effect → good mixing
- Inherent problem: surface area increases dramatically as layers increase → higher degradation potential

### 7.3 Stacked Dies (1989 onwards)

**Introduced by Brampton Engineering, 1989:**
- Layers arranged vertically (stacked) vs. concentric cylinders
- Each layer module has flat spiral distribution system
- Binary distribution: polymer divided 1→2→4→8 equal streams before spirals
- Flat spiral distribution system → circumferentially uniform radial flow

**Key advantages:**
- Minimized each layer's wetted surface area → minimized residence time → improved purge characteristics
- Modular design: easily modify/add/swap layers
- Individual modules can run at different temperatures → avoid thermal degradation of sensitive materials
- Successfully scaled to 10+ discrete layers

**Current state-of-the-art blown film dies:** Up to 10 layers (stacked design); 5-layer barrier dies very common.

### 7.4 Interfacial Instability

**Problem:** Interface between coextruded materials can become irregular or wavy → reduced optical quality.

**Fig 7-10 illustration:** Two-layer film with carbon black in one layer shows dark "bands" or "waves" → erratic periodic flow behavior.

**Understanding evolved with:**
- Commercial flow simulation software (Virtual Extrusion Laboratory, Compuplast)
- Design criteria based on material rheology and flow ratios

**Remedies:** Similar to flat die coextrusion (see Chapter 5):
- Reduce interfacial shear stress
- Match/mismatch layer viscosities strategically
- Reduce output rate
- Increase skin layer thickness
- Use appropriate die geometry

## Chapter 8: Process Engineering

**Author:** Eric Hatfield (MDO Engineering)

### 8.1 What Is Process Engineering?

**Process engineer's role:** Takes raw materials → sets operating conditions → runs them through manufacturing equipment → delivers product with desired customer properties, in the most economic manner.

Key distinction: Process engineer sees equipment + materials as **one entity in a state of flux** (like a doctor adjusting conditions for a healthy patient).

- Usually reports to **Operations** (not Engineering or R&D)
- Focus: transforming raw materials → finished product most economically with highest quality
- In short: **Make more of a higher quality product for less money**

**Distinction from process control:** Process control maintains a set point; process engineer **selects** the set point.

### 8.2 Tools of Process Engineers

- **Engineering principles:** Every phenomenon has a scientific reason; not art
- **Statistics:** Moving averages, data smoothing, frequency analysis of fluctuations → find root causes
- **Operator "interrogation":** Operators see symptoms; process engineer finds scientific root causes
- **Data interrogation:** Sort through conflicting data to find what's important
- **Past experience:** Invaluable for recognizing recurring problems
- **Knowledge of critical fitness for use (FFU) properties:** Prioritize which problem matters most
- **Open, inquiring mind:** No prejudice; continuously look for problems even when running well

### 8.3 Examples of Process Engineer Value

- Prioritize order of process changes
- Leadership and hands-on training to operators/supervisors
- Translate management goals to floor level
- Make R&D and new products work commercially (line speed, yield, reclaim inclusion, extruder grade harmonization)
- Bridge between R&D and plant culture
- Implement new technologies on plant floor
- Help QC fix quality problems quickly

### 8.4 Conclusion

The process engineer ties together: project engineer (installs equipment) + product engineer (selects materials) + maintenance engineer (keeps equipment running) = commercially viable product at the right cost and quality.

## Chapter 9: Blown Film, Cast Film and Lamination Processes

**Author:** John R. Wagner Jr. (Crescent Associates, Inc.)

### 9.1 Blown Film

**Definition:** Major processing technique for producing a biaxially melt-drawn film. Air pressure creates transverse direction (TD) draw; higher haul-off speed creates machine direction (MD) draw.

**Markets served:**
- Industrial films and bags
- Agricultural and construction films
- Barrier films
- Stretch films
- PVC cling films
- Laminating films
- Can liners
- High barrier small tube systems

**Polymers processed:** PE (dominant), PP, PVC, PS, EVA, EVOH, PA, PU

**Components of a coextruded blown film line:**
1. Resin feed system
2. Extruders (2–9 for multilayer)
3. Coextrusion die
4. Air ring (external cooling)
5. Internal pressure control (bubble diameter)
6. Collapsing frame
7. Haul-off roll (sets MD draw)
8. Treatment system (corona)
9. Winder

**Key design features for quality film:**
- Efficient, properly sized resin handling and feed system
- Efficient screw design: uniform/stable melt temperature, stable pressure, high output
- Optimized die: good layer control, thickness uniformity, ease of maintenance/durability
- Air rings with excellent cooling control and uniformity
- Automated web handling for efficiency and reduced changeover times
- Modular design for product changeovers
- Integrated, intuitive, operator-friendly control systems

**Common multilayer structures:** 2–9 extruders making 2–17 layers. 4-extruder system commonly makes symmetrical 7-layer [ABCDCBA] structures. Production: 100–10,000 PPH.

### 9.2 Cast Film

**Key difference from blown film:** Quenches molten extrudate on chilled steel roller(s) after die exit.

**Film vs. sheet distinction:** 0.010" (10 mils / 250 μm) — film < 0.010", sheet > 0.010"

**Process:** Melt pipes → multilayer die (with feedblock) → chill roll (solidification) → corona treatment → gauging system → slitting → winder. Trim recycled to extruder.

**Advantages over blown film:**
- Much higher output rates → advantage for large volume single-use films (e.g., stretch film)

**Disadvantages:**
- Fixed width (no bubble size adjustment)
- Less flexibility for different widths on same machine

**Sheet line:** Slower line speeds, multiple chill rolls for heat removal.

### 9.3 Extrusion Coating and Lamination

**Extrusion coating:** Single substrate + extrudate → melt coats surface → quenched on chill roll.

**Extrusion lamination:** Extrudate sandwiched between two substrates (auxiliary substrate comes from right onto chill roll).

**Typical products:**
- Lidding stock, candy wrappers, snack food bags, medical packaging
- Condiment packages, soup sachets, toothpaste tubes, cable wrap

**Requirements:**
- Excellent melt quality
- Proper configuration to fit products to be made
- Good gauge control
- Automation for quality and repeatability
- Flexible design for new products
- Intuitive, updatable control systems

## Chapter 10: Machine Direction Oriented Film Technology

**Author:** Eric Hatfield (MDO Engineering)

### 10.1 Overview

**Machine direction orientation (MDO):** Film stretched in MD only (vs. simultaneous biaxial orientation in both MD and TD).

**Equipment:** Stack of 8–12 separately driven rolls in side frames; rolls heated/cooled in separate temperature zones.

### 10.2 Four Sections of MDO Machine

1. **Preheat section (PH-1, PH-2):** Raise film temperature uniformly to orientation temperature without hard wrinkles. PH-1 has nip roll for incoming web tension control.

2. **Draw section:** Film heated to orientation temperature, then stretched between draw rolls. Draw rolls are **smaller diameter** than preheat rolls → tangent points closer together → narrower stretch zone. Gap between draw rolls is adjustable. Film can be stretched up to 10× or more. Slow roll and fast roll both have nip rolls for tension control.

3. **Annealing section (A-1, A-2):** Heat-sets the film ("locks in" property changes). Controls how much film will shrink when later exposed to heat. A-1 has nip roll for tension control.

4. **Cooling section (C-1, C-2):** Cools film to near ambient temperature.

### 10.3 Molecular Mechanism of MDO

Before orientation: ordered crystals (plates with molecules tilted 34.4° to film surface) tied together by amorphous tie molecules with random amorphous regions.

**Phase 1:** Random amorphous molecules begin aligning in orientation direction.
**Phase 2:** Tie molecules become fully extended → tilt/angle of molecules within crystals changes → molecules align parallel to film surface.
**Phase 3:** Crystals begin to unravel → film eventually breaks.

Different resins have different phase transition points → different draw ratios → different properties. In coextrusion, properties can be optimized per layer using different resins.

### 10.4 Properties Improved by MDO

**Optics:**
- Haze decreases, gloss increases with draw ratio up to ~6×
- Clear see-through clarity dramatically improved

**Tensiles:**
- MD tensile strength at break greatly increased (LLDPE example shows large increase)
- TD tensile strength slightly reduced BUT TD elongation to break still several hundred percent → wrapped item elongates both MD and TD → both strengths utilized → much tougher package

**Stiffness:**
- Secant modulus increases 2–3× (LLDPE) to ~4× and above (HMW-HDPE)
- Stiffness improvement in both MD and TD directions

**Barrier (moisture and oxygen):**

| Film | No MDO WVTR | With MDO WVTR |
|---|---|---|
| LLDPE 2 mil | 0.95 g/100 in²·24h·atm | 0.56 (×1.7 improvement) |
| HDPE 1.5 mil, 6:1 draw | 0.27 | 0.14 (×1.9 improvement) |

Similar improvements in oxygen transmission rates. HDPE moisture barrier can be doubled.

**Other improvements:** Controlled shrink levels, dead fold, film toughness/puncture resistance.

## Chapter 11: Oriented Film Technology

**Author:** Jürgen Breil (Brückner Maschinenbau GmbH)

### 11.1 Introduction

**Biaxially oriented films:** Stretched in both MD and TD → meet demands of modern flexible packaging:
- Excellent barrier properties against gases
- Good quality seals
- High-gloss, transparent thin film
- Excellent printability
- Good material utilization
- Fulfillment of high-speed packaging line requirements
- Environmental compatibility (polyolefin recyclability, optimal raw material yield)

**Orientation improves (example BOPP vs. cast PP):**
- Young's modulus (MD and TD)
- Tensile strength
- Optical properties (haze, gloss)
- Barrier properties (O₂ and moisture)

**Mechanism:** Orientation of molecule chains: random (non-oriented) → clear chain orientation (oriented). Semicrystalline plastics additionally augment crystallinity → improved mechanical values.

### 11.2 Orienting Technologies

**Stretching directions/processes:**
- MD only → MDO (Chapter 10)
- TD only → TDO (transverse direction orienter; film fixed at ends, stretched in oven)
- Sequential biaxial → MDO then TDO (most prevalent)
- Simultaneous biaxial → tenter with simultaneous clip motion OR double bubble process

**Sequential stretching (MDO + TDO):**
- Dominant for all types of packaging films, tapes, labels, industrial films
- For BOPP: Cast resin → MDO → TDO → gauging → treatment → winder
- Typical temperatures: melt in extruder → quench on cast roll → reheat for MDO → anneal → reheat for TDO → anneal → cool → wind
- State-of-art BOPP: working width 10 m, speed 530 m/min, output 6,000 kg/h
- Future: 7+ tons/hour lines in development

**Simultaneous stretching (LISIM® technology):**
- Individually driven clips with linear motor technology
- Larger usable stretch ratio range than sequential (especially in MD)
- Allows identical MD/TD ratios or higher MD stretch
- Unrestricted relaxation in both directions → controlled shrink
- Contact-free → avoids roll limitations → better optical quality
- Better for materials that cannot be stretched sequentially (crystallinity formed in first MD step)
- Low temperature sealing polymers not processable in standard MDO (stick to rolls) → possible in LISIM®
- Advantages: high productivity, high flexibility, high reliability

**Double bubble process:** Initial tube extruded → rapidly cooled → reheated to stretch temperature → synchronous increase in draw-off speed + bubble expansion = simultaneous orientation.

### 11.4 BOPP Films

- **Worldwide consumption:** >6 million tons/year; dominant share; growing ~7%/year
- **Thickness range:** 3 μm (capacitor film) to 180 μm (synthetic paper)
- **Packaging range:** 15–35 μm most common
- **Structures:** Monolayer → 3-layer (most common) → 5-layer (growing trend) → 7-layer
- **3-layer standard:** PP homopolymer core + PP copolymer outer layers (lower MP for heat sealing)
- **5-layer advantages:** Better optical properties (gloss, haze, transparency, opacity); cost reduction (expensive additives only in thin intermediate layers)
- **White opaque (cavitated) BOPP:** Inorganic additives (CaCO₃) in polymer matrix → flaking/separation during MDO → small cavities (vacuoles) during TDO → light refraction → pearl effect + density reduction → same raw material gives larger thickness
- **Synthetic paper:** Cavitated BOPP, density 0.6–0.9 g/cm³; thickness 50–1000 μm; applications: pressure-sensitive labels, posters, maps, shopping bags, phone cards, menus

### 11.5 BOPET Films

- **Worldwide consumption:** ~2 million tons/year; second most common; growing 4–5%/year
- **Applications:** ~40% packaging; remainder: technical (capacitors, electrical insulation, magnetic tapes historically; now LCD screen components growing)
- **Properties:** High mechanical strength, good temperature/chemical resistance, dimensional stability, adjustable COF, excellent optical clarity, good printability
- **Process variants:** MD/TD (dominant), TD/MD, MD/TD/MD, simultaneous (thin capacitor films)
- **Coextrusion:** Sealable or matte surfaces; inorganic additives in thin outer layers for COF
- **Metallized BOPET:** Oxygen permeation < 1 cm³/m²·d·bar → food applications
- **Common laminate structure:** BOPET (reverse printed) / aluminum foil (barrier) / PE sealant film (coffee wrapping example)
- **Non-packaging:** Thermal-transfer films (bar code printers), capacitors, electrical insulation (0.5–350 μm), optical films for LCDs

### 11.6 BOPA Films (Biaxially Oriented Polyamide)

- **Properties:** Excellent toughness, good oxygen barrier, excellent puncture resistance
- **Sequential and simultaneous processes used**
- Good oxygen and aroma barrier; often used in coextrusion with PE sealant layers
- Applications: meat, cheese, processed food packaging (high abuse resistance)

### 11.7 Other Oriented Films

- **BOPS (biaxially oriented polystyrene):** Food packaging (cups, trays)
- **BOPE (biaxially oriented polyethylene):** Double bubble process; shrink bags, sausage casings
- **BOPVC (biaxially oriented PVC):** Shrink films

**Global biaxially oriented film market:** BOPP ~66%, BOPET ~25%, others (PS, PVC, PA) remainder of 12 million tons/year (2008).

## Chapter 12: Polymer Blending for Packaging Applications

**Author:** Barry A. Morris (DuPont Packaging & Industrial Polymers) [with David Strutt contributions]

### 12.1 Introduction and Objectives

**Why blend?**
- Achieve properties not achievable in a single polymer
- Improve processing (e.g., blend LDPE into LLDPE for better bubble stability)
- Reduce cost
- Modify barrier, mechanical, optical, or thermal properties

**Key blending considerations:**
- Miscibility (thermodynamics: Flory-Huggins theory, χ parameter)
- Compatibility (interfacial tension, morphology development)
- Blend morphology: matrix/dispersed phase, co-continuous, lamellar
- Processing equipment: single-screw vs. twin-screw extruder

### 12.2 Thermodynamics of Blending

**Flory-Huggins theory:** For two high-MW polymers to be miscible, interaction parameter χ must be very small (negative or near zero) because entropy contribution of mixing is negligible for high MW.

**Most polymer pairs are immiscible** → must form controlled morphology.

**Interfacial tension** controls equilibrium morphology; lower interfacial tension → finer dispersion → compatibilizer can reduce it.

**Compatibilizers:** Block copolymers, graft copolymers, reactive compatibilizers (e.g., maleic anhydride-grafted resins). Reduce interfacial tension → finer morphology → improved properties.

### 12.3 Blend Morphology Development

**Morphology types:**
- **Matrix-dispersed particle** (spheres, ellipsoids)
- **Fibrillar** (elongated droplets)
- **Lamellar** (alternating layers)
- **Co-continuous** (both phases interconnected)

**Droplet breakup and coalescence:**
- Critical capillary number `Ca_cr = η_matrix × γ̇ × R / Γ` (Γ = interfacial tension)
- Breakup occurs when Ca > Ca_cr
- Coalescence competes with breakup → final morphology depends on balance

**Processing equipment:**
- **Single-screw extruder:** Limited dispersive mixing; mostly distributive mixing; specific mixing sections (barrier screw, Maddock, Dulmage) can improve
- **Twin-screw extruder:** Better dispersive and distributive mixing; better control of morphology; more versatile; used for reactive blending and compounding

### 12.4 Key Blends in Flexible Packaging

**LLDPE/LDPE blends:**
- LLDPE provides strength and toughness
- LDPE provides melt strength and bubble stability (LCB → extensional viscosity)
- Typical ratios: 70:30 to 80:20 LLDPE:LDPE for blown film
- Miscible in the melt; slight incompatibility in solid state

**PE/EVA blends:** Softer, more flexible; improved sealability at lower temperatures

**PA/EVOH blends:** Improved oxygen and moisture barrier; better flexibility vs. monolayer EVOH

**PE/nanocomposites:** Clay (montmorillonite) dispersed at nanoscale → improved barrier, stiffness, flame retardancy. Key: exfoliation of clay platelets. Nanocor nanomers used.

### 12.5 Blending Equipment and Processes

**Blending processes:**
- Melt blending (most common for flexible packaging): both components melted, mixed in extruder
- Dry blending (pellets/powder): premix before feeding to extruder
- Masterbatch addition: high concentration of additive/modifier in carrier resin, added in small amounts

**Single-screw mixing sections:**
- Maddock (Union Carbide) mixing section
- Dulmage mixing section
- Barrier screws (separate melt from solid)
- Wave screws
- Pineapple, spiral, and elongational mixing screws

**Twin-screw extruder (co-rotating) for compounding:**
- Better temperature control
- Vacuum degassing
- Multiple feeding ports
- Self-wiping design
- Preferred for reactive blending and complex formulations

## Chapter 13: Water- and Solvent-Based Coating Technology

**Authors:** Edgar B. Gutoff (Consulting Chemical Engineer) and Edward D. Cohen, PhD (AIMCAL Technical Consultant)

### 13.1 Introduction

**Role of coating:** Many packaging substrates cannot meet all functional requirements alone. Coatings add:
- Oxygen and moisture barrier
- Adhesion promotion
- Antistatic properties
- Printability, aesthetics, durability
- Release coatings

**Advanced capability:** Simultaneously coat 12 or more layers (slide coating / precision curtain coating).

### 13.2 Coating Solution Ingredients

All coatings contain:
1. **Binder:** Non-volatile solid (polymer) that binds functional ingredients to form film. May be dissolved or dispersed as latex.
2. **Solvent:** Dissolves binder, suspends/disperses insoluble components.
3. **Dispersed functional particles:** Pigments, silver halides, iron/chromium oxides, carbon blacks.
4. **Additives:** Surfactants, dispersants, plasticizers, UV absorbers, biocides, cross-linking agents, conductive materials.
5. **Surface texture particles:** Silica or hard latex spheres for roughness/anti-blocking.

### 13.3 Coating Machine Components

1. **Solution preparation:** Kettles, high/low shear mixers, scales, filtration. Separate batch operation.
2. **Unwind stand:** Holds uncoated substrate roll.
3. **Web transport system:** Drive rolls, tension control, edge guides; speed range 0.01–15 m/s; widths up to 3.7 m+.
4. **Surface treatment:** Flame, corona, gas plasma before coating applicator.
5. **Coating applicator:** Applies coating solution to moving web.
6. **Dryer:** Hot air impingement (most common), flotation dryers (also heat uncoated side), infrared heaters.
7. **Post-treatment:** UV or e-beam cross-linking, moisture addition (steam), lamination.
8. **Rewind stand:** Winds coated product into rolls.

**Laboratory coaters:** 15–60 cm wide, 0.05–0.25 m/s; for R&D, scale-up, small volume production.

### 13.4 Coating Applicator Classifications

**Classification by metering mechanism:**

| Class | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Self-metered | Free meniscus controls coating weight | Dip, meniscus/bead roll, forward roll |
| Doctored | Doctor device removes excess | Air knife, blade/knife coaters, Mayer rod |
| Pre-metered | All fluid fed to die is applied; coverage = flow rate/speed | Slot die, extrusion coater, slide, precision curtain |
| Hybrid | Combines pre-metered + doctored | Gravure |

### 13.5 Major Coating Methods

**Dip coaters:**
- Web passes under applicator roll submerged in coating pan
- Thickness depends on: fluid properties, coating speed, withdrawal angle
- Viscosity 20–2,000 cP; wet thickness 10–200 μm; speed 0.5–7.5 m/s; uniformity ±10%

**Forward roll coaters:**
- Applicator roll rotates same direction as substrate; fluid splits at nip
- Susceptible to ribbing defect (Ca > critical value)
- Viscosity 20–2,000 cP; wet 10–200 μm; speed 0.5–7.5 m/s; uniformity ±10%

**Reverse roll coaters:**
- Applicator roll rotates opposite to substrate direction; metering roll doctors excess
- Most widely used; extremely versatile; resistant to ribbing and cascade defects
- Viscosity 200–50,000 cP; wet 14–450 μm; speed 0.1–8.5 m/s; uniformity ±2–10%

**Mayer rod (wire-wound rod) coaters:**
- Wire-wound rod doctors excess coating; ~100+ years in use; still widely used
- Coverage ~10.7% of wire diameter (actual less: 50–10.7%)
- Rod rotates (usually reverse to substrate) to equalize wear
- Viscosity 50–1,000 cP; wet 4–80 μm; speed 0.05–5 m/s; uniformity ±7–10%

**Air knife coaters:**
- Two modes: Precision mode (low pressure, quality uniform coating) and Squeegee mode (high pressure, for porous supports)
- Precision: viscosity 1–50 cP; wet 1–200 μm; speed 0.2–2 m/s; uniformity ±5%
- Squeegee: viscosity 5–500 cP; speed 0.6–10 m/s; uniformity ±5%
- Disadvantages: noise, contamination, spray, viscosity limitations

**Knife coaters (knife-over-roll):**
- Thick, rigid knife doctors off excess; simple, inexpensive
- Fills in depressions → flat surface even on rough substrates
- Viscosity 50–40,000 cP; wet 10–750 μm; speed 1.7–25 m/s; uniformity ±10%

**Blade coaters:**
- Thin (0.2–0.5 mm) spring steel blades (rigid or flexible); wide variety of configurations
- Flexible blade: coverage decreases as force increases; used extensively on pigmented coatings (paper industry)
- Viscosity 50–40,000 cP; wet 10–750 μm; speed 1.7–25 m/s; uniformity ±10%

**Slot die coaters:**
- Coating solution exits slot between die lips; forms coating bead between lips and substrate
- Vacuum (up to 1,000 Pa) stabilizes bead → thinner coatings possible
- Can coat 2–3 simultaneous layers
- Viscosity 15–20,000 cP; wet 10–250 μm; speed 0.02–8.5 m/s; uniformity ±2%
- Best uniformity of pre-metered methods

**Extrusion coaters:**
- High viscosity material (>50,000 cP), molten polymer at elevated temperatures
- Extruded ribbon laid on substrate (does not wet die lips — differs from slot die)
- Wide gap between die lips and substrate
- Used for food packaging requiring vapor/oxygen barrier and heat sealability

**Gravure coaters:**
- Cells on gravure roll fill in coating pan; doctor blade removes excess; cells transfer to substrate
- Cell patterns: pyramid, quad, tri-helix, channel types
- Cell volume determines coverage (partly pre-metered)
- Gravure patterns: standard, elongated, or open (low coverage, high coverage, or uniform)
- MicroGravure™: smaller roll, finer cells; coater at top of roll
- Widely used; excellent for low-coverage, precise applications

**Slide coaters (multilayer):**
- Multiple layers flow down inclined planes (slides) → merge at bottom → applied to substrate
- Can simultaneously coat 12+ layers
- Preferred for many-layer thin coatings (pharmaceutical, photographic, batteries)
- Must maintain curtain integrity → higher minimum coverage

**Precision curtain coaters:**
- Similar to slide coating
- Curtain of coating falls freely onto substrate
- Minimum flow rate needed for curtain integrity → inherently high-speed process

### 13.6 Substrate Properties

Key substrate properties affecting coating:
- **Porosity:** Open (paper, fabric) vs. closed (PET, PE, PP, PVC, cellophane) — affects penetration, drying, uniformity
- **Thickness and stiffness:** Very stiff (metals), average (most plastics 25–250 μm), low stiffness (<15 μm)
- **Wettability and adhesion:** Substrate surface tension should be ~5 dyn/cm higher than coating; flame/corona/plasma treatment improves adhesion; subbing/primer layers

### 13.7 Coating Defects and Limits

**Coatability limits:**
- High-speed limit: Air entrainment under coating → coating quality suffers (applies to all coaters)
- Low-speed limit: Fluid runs back; thick coating not carried off

**Ribbing:** Sinusoidal coating thickness variation (machine direction orientation like comb). Occurs in forward roll when capillary number Ca = ηU/σ > critical value.

**Cascade (seashore):** Air entrapment defect in reverse roll metering.

## Chapter 14: Vacuum Metallizing for Flexible Packaging

**Author:** Charles A. Bishop (VacuumCoatingTraining.com, AIMCAL)

### 14.1 Introduction and Applications

**History:** First vacuum roll coating commercial operations date to the 1940s. Used for:
- Oxygen and water vapor barrier improvement
- Attractive metallic appearance
- Light barrier
- Holographic security features
- Microwave susceptors
- Antistatic packaging
- Electrical applications (capacitors, bus bars)

**Major applications:**
- **Snack food bags** (potato chips, nuts, salty snacks) — metallized OPP or PET
- **Confectionary** (chocolate, candy wrappers) — metallized films
- **Coffee, tea packaging** — metallized laminates
- **Pharmaceutical blister packs** — aluminum foil; metallized films growing
- **Security/authentication** — holograms on packaging; prevents counterfeiting
- **Decorative labels** — metallized paper or film
- **Microwave packaging** — patterned metallization for heating elements or fuse susceptors

### 14.2 Vacuum Deposition Process

**Thermal evaporation (most common for aluminum):**
- Aluminum heated above evaporation temperature in vacuum (~10⁻⁴ torr)
- Evaporated aluminum travels to and condenses on film substrate
- Film on roll unwinds, passes over evaporation source, rewound
- Very thin coating (typically 25–50 nm for packaging barrier; measured by optical density)

**Electron beam deposition:**
- Used for higher-temperature materials (SiOx, AlOx, other oxides)
- Electron beam focused on target material → evaporation → deposition

**Plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD):**
- For depositing SiOx (silicon oxide) or other compounds
- Provides transparent barriers while maintaining printability/visibility of contents

**Key factors for high-quality metallized films:**
1. **Substrate surface cleanliness:** Dust, oligomers, slip agents can all interfere. Minimize surface contamination. Corona/plasma treatment before metallization.
2. **Surface chemistry/energy:** High surface energy → better aluminum wetting → more continuous coating → lower defect density → better barrier at thinnest coating thickness
3. **Surface smoothness:** Rough surfaces → more pinholes → worse barrier
4. **Coating thickness uniformity:** Controlled by evaporation rate, substrate speed, evaporation source length

**Optical density (OD):** Common measure of metallization level; for food packaging barrier: OD typically 2.5–3.5 (transmittance 0.3–3%). Higher OD = more metal = better barrier but higher cost.

### 14.3 Barrier Properties of Metallized Films

**Aluminum barrier performance:**
- OTR: < 1 cm³/m²·d·bar achievable (vs. 90 for BOPET alone)
- WVTR: comparable improvement
- Light barrier: critical for light-sensitive products

**Silicon oxide (SiOx) and aluminum oxide (AlOx) coatings:**
- **Transparent** → product visible in package
- Good barrier properties (OTR < 1 cm³/m²·d·bar)
- Compatible with microwave use (unlike aluminum)
- Better recyclability in some systems
- More expensive to deposit than aluminum

**Factors degrading barrier:**
- Flex cracking (especially in laminated structures)
- Inadequate aging/oxidation of aluminum (oxide layer needed for handling)
- Surface contamination during or after deposition
- Pinholes from substrate surface defects

**Good quality barrier starts with:**
- High-quality substrates
- Minimized film surface contamination
- Optimized surface chemistry
- Maximized surface smoothness

### 14.4 Substrate Preparation

**Surface treatment before metallization:**
- Corona discharge: increases surface energy; adds polar functional groups
- Plasma treatment: more controllable; can use various gases (Ar, N₂, O₂)
- Flame treatment: older method; still used

**Surface contamination sources:**
- Slip additives (FA amides): major problem; migrate to surface, prevent good adhesion of metal
- Oligomers: low-MW polymer species that migrate out
- Dust: scratches and pinholes
- Coatings/lacquers: may interfere or help depending on formulation

**Metallizable additive formulations:**
- Specific grades of BOPP/BOPET designed for metallization with reduced or substituted slip agents
- Hydrotalcite instead of calcium stearate (hydrotalcite better for metallization adhesion)

### 14.5 Aging, Oxidation, and Adhesion

**Aluminum oxidation:**
- Fresh aluminum reacts rapidly with oxygen → aluminum oxide surface layer forms
- Aging before use: allows oxide to toughen → better protection during handling
- Oxide layer: improves lamination adhesion; protects against further degradation

**Adhesion of metal to substrate:**
- Critical for barrier integrity in laminated structures
- Good adhesion prevents delamination or flex cracking
- Oxygen barrier can be compromised if metal layer delaminates

### 14.6 Market Trends

**Growing:** Barrier packaging replacing aluminum foil in many applications; transparent barrier films (SiOx, AlOx); holograms for authentication; metallized papers for labels.

**Declining:** Metallized paper volumes flat/declining; magnetic recording media (replaced by optical/electronic storage) — formerly major application for BOPET.

**Active/intelligent packaging trend:** Scavenger-coated films (palladium added during e-beam deposition of silica → getters O₂); more integral to lamination structure.

### 15.3 Mechanical Properties

**Law of mixtures estimate:**
```
M = [Σ(tᵢ × Mᵢ)] / [Σtᵢ]
```
where M = film property, tᵢ = layer thickness, Mᵢ = polymer property per unit thickness.

**Limitations:** Does not account for layer interactions (positive or negative). Example: LDPE core in LLDPE/core/LLDPE → linear relationship (law of mixtures valid). HDPE core in same structure → non-linear (HDPE brittleness causes stress concentration → worse than law of mixtures predicts).

**Film stiffness:**
- Function of stiffness (modulus), thickness, and **position** of each layer
- Outer layers have greatest impact on stiffness (like I-beam principle)
- Separating stiff layers → efficient stiffness building → enables down-gauging

### 15.4 Barrier Properties

**Permeation mechanism (3-step):**
1. Molecule dissolves into film structure
2. Molecule diffuses through film layers
3. Molecule desorbs on opposite side

**Permeability:** `P = D × S` (diffusivity × solubility coefficient)

**Multilayer permeability (series resistance model):**
```
1/P = f₁/P₁ + f₂/P₂ + ... + fₙ/Pₙ
```
where fᵢ = layer thickness fraction, Pᵢ = layer permeability coefficient.

**Actual transmission rate:**
```
TM = P / tₜ
```
where tₜ = total film thickness.

**Hot tack:** Seal strength while still in molten state. Critical for VFFS (vertical form-fill-seal) machines — product weight on bottom seal while still hot. Also for HFFS gusseted areas.

Hot tack mechanism: polymer diffusion/penetration (increases with temperature) vs. melt strength (decreases with temperature) → maximum at intermediate temperature.

**Ionomer advantage:** Broad hot tack range; excellent seal integrity vs. POP (narrow hot tack range).

### 15.6 Adhesion in Coextruded Structures

**Interlayer adhesion depends on:**
- Material pair compatibility (thermodynamic driving force)
- Process parameters (temperature, cooling rate, draw-down)
- Time between die exit and solidification
- Die/feedblock design

**Tie resins (coextrudable adhesives):**
- Based on modified polyolefins (MAH-grafted) → chemical bonding to polar layers (PA, EVOH, PVDC)
- DuPont Bynel®, Mitsui Admer®, and others
- Critical for PA/EVOH/polyolefin structures

**Interlayer adhesion issues:**
- Insufficient adhesion → delamination during use
- Post-die processing time affects peel strength (molecular diffusion across interface)
- Thicker structures require longer dwell for heat seal

**Lamination adhesion:**
- Solvent/water-based adhesives (polyurethane or PEI based)
- Extrusion lamination: molten PE layer as adhesive
- Adhesion can be compromised by fatty acids and other chemicals migrating from food contact

### 15.7 Specific Film Applications

**Barrier cereal liners:** HDPE/tie/EVOH/tie/EVA (or similar structure)
- HDPE core (moisture barrier, stiffness)
- EVOH (oxygen barrier)
- EVA (low-temperature sealant)

**Meat and cheese packaging (high barrier):**
- Typically laminations with BOPET or BOPP outer layer (reverse printed) + barrier layer (PA/EVOH or metallized) + PE sealant inner layer
- Hot tack and seal-through-contamination critical (product juices on seal area)

**Stretch films:**
- Often monolayer LLDPE or multilayer with m-LLDPE/VLDPE skin layers (high cling) + LLDPE core
- Cast film preferred for higher output

**Heavy duty bags:**
- HMW-HDPE / LLDPE coextrusions
- High tear and puncture resistance
- MDO HDPE/LLDPE for improved stiffness and barrier

## Chapter 16: Multilayer Oriented Films

**Author:** Jürgen Breil (Brückner Maschinenbau GmbH)

### 16.1 Introduction

Monolayer biaxial orientation meets some needs; **multilayer oriented films** meet diverse simultaneous requirements:
- Mechanical properties
- Sliding/friction characteristics
- Sealing properties
- Surface treatment/energy for printing and coating adhesion
- Hot tack
- Antistatic activity
- Metallizing properties
- Optical properties (haze, gloss, whiteness)
- Stiffness
- Barrier properties

High barrier layers (EVOH) require tie layers → results in 5- or 7-layer structures.

**Multilayer methods:**
1. In-line coating (liquid or extrusion coating between MDO and TDO)
2. In-line lamination (second film laminated before stretching)
3. **Coextrusion** (dominant method — most flexible, most cost-effective)

### 16.2 Technology for Multilayer Oriented Films

**Coextrusion line for 5-layer BOPP (Fig. 16-3):**
- Main extruder + twin-screw additive feeder + melt pump + melt filter
- 2 single-screw coextruders + 2 twin-screw coextruders
- Melt flows merged in multichannel die or laminar flow adapters
- Multichannel dies: most common; precise layer distribution across working width; coat-hanger distribution per layer; film edge trim areas without coextruded layers (enables inline edge trim recycling for BOPP)

**7-layer multichannel die:** ABCDCBA configuration; each polymer's channel optimized for its viscosity and desired layer width.

**Twin-screw extruder advantages in orientation lines:**
- Better homogenization of additives and recycled/edge trim material
- Lower specific energy
- Better degasification
- Self-purification → shorter product switchover
- For BOPET: eliminates complex resin drying

**Process window coordination:** Each polymer has different stretch ratios and temperatures; usable process windows must not overlap → careful selection of resin combination.

### 16.3 BOPP Multilayer Structures

**3-layer (introduced 1970s):** PP homopolymer core + PP copolymer outer skin layers (lower MP → heat sealable). Antiblock and slip additives in outer layers. Seal initiation temperature requirements → low-temperature copolymers.

**5-layer benefits:**
- Optical: high opacity (white opaque), high gloss, matte surfaces via specific masterbatches in intermediate layers
- Cost: higher recycled material content in main intermediate layers without impairing optics; expensive additives concentrated in thin intermediate layers
- Sealing: low seal initiation temperature (SIT) copolymer as intermediate layer over thick PP copolymer → very high seal strength + sealed seam rigidity

**5-layer Low SIT BOPP film:**
- Structure: [skin (copolymer)/intermediate (low SIT copolymer)/core (homopolymer)/intermediate/skin]
- Very high seal strength and sealed seam rigidity
- Critical for product protection on high-speed packaging lines

**5-layer UHB (ultra-high barrier) metallized BOPP film:**
- Surface layer: high surface energy polymer (PA, EVOH, etc.) → better aluminum adhesion + uniformity
- Tie layer: bonds high-energy surface layer to PP core
- Core: PP homopolymer
- Fourth layer: PP (bonded with tie)
- Fifth layer: heat sealable copolymer
- Result: **50× better oxygen barrier** vs. standard metallized BOPP

### 16.4 7-Layer Transparent Barrier BOPP Films

**Need:** Transparent (not metallized) barrier films → see-through packaging.

**Solution:** 7-layer EVOH-containing BOPP structure:
- Structure: [skin A / tie / core B / EVOH / core B / tie / skin A] (symmetric)
- EVOH content: not more than 33% ethylene for best barrier vs. stretchability balance
- **Result:** O₂ permeation < 2 cm³/m²·d·bar with 2 μm EVOH layer → competitive with off-line coated films

**EVOH type selection:**
- Higher ethylene content → easier orientation, higher stretching ratios, but poorer barrier
- For simultaneous orientation: all EVOH types (24–47% ethylene) can be stretched
- For sequential: higher ethylene content preferred to avoid issues from first MD stretch crystallinity

**Symmetric structure advantage:** Less curling tendency compared to asymmetric structures.

**Additional BOPET and BOPA multilayers:** Same coextrusion principles apply; barrier, sealing, friction, and optical layers all possible.

### 16.5 Market Trends for Multilayer Oriented Films

**2008 worldwide capacity:** >12 million tons biaxially stretched films (~66% BOPP, ~25% BOPET).

**Trend 1 — Cost Efficiency:**
- Downgaging enabled by multilayer (same functional performance at lower total thickness)
- High mechanical properties + excellent friction → high packaging speeds
- Many laminates → intense efforts to combine all properties in one coextruded oriented film → reduces costly lamination step
- Film stretching lines will become increasingly complex/diverse

**Trend 2 — Consumer Requirements:**
- Easy open and reclosable → composites with low tear resistance and reclosable features
- See-through requirements → transparent barrier films (coextruded with EVOH layers)
- Long shelf life → transparent lidding films for sausage/cheese in deep-drawn trays

**Trend 3 — Sustainability:**
- Energy consumption labeling → pressure on complex multi-step processes → advantage for coextruded single-step processes
- Replacing aluminum foil (more energy to produce) with plastic barrier films
- Renewable sourced materials (PLA) → coextrusion + orientation enables needed properties for PLA packaging
- Legal requirements on marking and material use will increase importance of sustainability

## Key Formulas Reference

### Crystallinity
- Weight % crystallinity: `Xw = [(ρ - ρa)/(ρc - ρa)] × (ρc/ρ) × 100`  (ρa = 0.853, ρc = 1.000 g/cc for PE)
- Volume % crystallinity: `Xv = [(1/ρ - 1/ρa)/(1/ρc - 1/ρa)] × 100`

### Rheology
- Viscosity: `η = σ/γ̇`
- Power law: `η = m × γ̇^(n-1)` or `σ = m × γ̇^n`
- Wall shear rate (tube): `γ̇w = 4Q/(πR³)` (apparent); Rabinowitsch corrected: `γ̇_true = [(3n+1)/(4n)] × (4Q/πR³)`
- Wall shear stress (tube): `σw = (ΔP/2L) × R`
- Temperature dependence: `η = η_ref × exp(-β × ΔT)`
- Zero-shear viscosity: `η₀ ≈ const × M_w^3.4`
- Trouton ratio (Newtonian): `η_e/η = 3`

### Barrier / Permeability
- Single polymer: `P = D × S`
- Multilayer (series resistance): `1/P_total = Σ(fᵢ/Pᵢ)`
- Transmission rate: `TM = P/t_total`

### Mechanical (Law of Mixtures for Multilayer Film)
- `M = Σ(tᵢ × Mᵢ) / Σtᵢ`

### Melt Index to Shear Rate (PE at 190°C)
- `γ̇ ≈ 2.4 × MI` (s⁻¹)
