## 1. The Role of Food Packaging

Packaging serves six core functions: containment, protection, preservation, information, convenience, and presentation. Less than 1% of packaged food goes to waste versus 10–20% of unpackaged food. The total systems approach to packaging optimisation means that increasing packaging cost can be justified if it reduces waste, improves logistics, or extends shelf life elsewhere in the chain.

A packaging strategy must balance technical requirements (barrier, seal integrity, shelf life), customer-valued characteristics (convenience, aesthetics), distribution needs (compression, vibration, climate), regulatory compliance, environmental responsibility, and cost.

## 2. Food Preservation & Processing

### 2.1 Thermal Processing
- Pasteurisation: mild heat (typically 72°C/15s for milk) kills vegetative pathogens but not all spores.
- Sterilisation: higher temperatures (e.g. 121°C for canning, 135°C+ UHT) achieve commercial sterility.
- Retort pouches process at 115–135°C under overpressure. Thin pouch cross-section (12–33 mm) enables rapid heat penetration — a 2 mm change in pouch thickness can shift the F₀ value by 1.5 min.

### 2.2 Chilling & Freezing
- Chilling (0–5°C) slows microbial growth; respiration rate of produce increases 3–4× per 10°C rise.
- Freezing stops microbial growth but does not sterilise. Freezer burn occurs from moisture migration inside poorly fitting packaging.

### 2.3 Drying & Water Activity
- Reducing water activity (aw) below critical thresholds inhibits specific organisms: bacteria generally below 0.90, yeasts below 0.87, moulds below 0.80.
- Dried products require moisture barrier packaging to prevent rehydration.

### 2.4 Chemical Preservation
- Curing (salt, nitrite), pickling (acid), smoking, and fermentation all lower aw or pH to inhibit spoilage.
- Packaging must be compatible with preservative chemistry (e.g. acidic products and metal corrosion).

### 2.5 Modified Atmosphere Packaging (MAP)
- Replaces air with controlled gas mixtures (CO₂, N₂, O₂) to extend shelf life.
- CO₂ inhibits aerobic bacteria (effective above 20%); N₂ is inert filler preventing pack collapse; O₂ maintains red meat colour (oxymyoglobin) at 70–80%.
- Equilibrium MAP for produce: film permeability matched to product respiration rate so atmosphere self-regulates.

## 3. Shelf Life & Spoilage Mechanisms

### 3.1 Chemical/Biochemical
- Lipid oxidation: the primary shelf-life limiter for fats, oils, nuts, snacks. Proceeds via free radical chain reaction (initiation → propagation → termination). Reducing O₂ below 1% and using antioxidants are key strategies.
- Enzymatic browning: polyphenol oxidase in cut fruits/vegetables. Controlled by low O₂, acidification, or sulfites.
- Ethylene (C₂H₄): plant hormone accelerating ripening. Concentrations as low as 0.1 ppm affect sensitive produce over time.

### 3.2 Microbiological
- Under ideal conditions, bacteria double every 20 min — one cell becomes 16 million in 8 hours.
- Key pathogens: C. botulinum (anaerobic, grows at ≥3°C), Listeria monocytogenes (grows at refrigeration temps), Salmonella, E. coli.
- Packaging role: maintain hermetic seal integrity post-thermal processing; control atmosphere to inhibit aerobic spoilage organisms.

### 3.3 Physical
- Moisture transfer: products gain or lose moisture toward equilibrium relative humidity (ERH). Packaging WVTR must be matched to product sensitivity.
- Light damage: UV and visible light catalyse oxidation (especially in dairy, oils, beer). Barrier via opacity, metallisation, or UV-absorbing materials.
- Physical damage: compression, vibration, impact during distribution. Seal areas and closures are most vulnerable.

### 3.4 Migration from Packaging
- Monomers, additives, plasticisers, and ink solvents can migrate from packaging into food.
- EU overall migration limit: 10 mg/dm² (or 60 mg/kg food). Specific migration limits exist for individual substances.
- Factors affecting migration: temperature, time, fat content of food, polymer type, and contact area.
- Residual solvents from printing/lamination inks are a key concern — must be below regulatory limits.

## 4. Packaging Materials

### 4.1 Metal Cans
- Steel (tinplate): three-piece welded or two-piece drawn. Tin coating provides corrosion protection and reducing environment.
- Aluminium: lightweight, excellent barrier, used for beverage cans (DWI process) and foil.
- Aluminium foil density: ~2.7 g/cm³. Provides absolute barrier to gases, moisture, and light when pinhole-free (typically ≥9 μm).

### 4.2 Glass
- Absolute barrier, chemically inert, reusable, recyclable. Density ~2.5 g/cm³.
- Limitations: weight, fragility, thermal shock sensitivity.

### 4.3 Plastics — Key Properties

| Polymer | Density (g/cm³) | Key Properties | Typical Use |
|---------|----------------|----------------|-------------|
| LDPE | 0.91–0.93 | Flexible, good moisture barrier, poor gas barrier | Bags, liners, extrusion coatings |
| HDPE | 0.94–0.96 | Stiffer, better moisture barrier than LDPE | Bottles, crates |
| PP | 0.90 | Heat resistant (up to ~130°C), good moisture barrier | Retortable trays, films, closures |
| PET | ~1.38–1.40 | Excellent clarity, strength, good gas barrier | Bottles, films, trays |
| PA (Nylon) | 1.13–1.15 | Tough, good gas/odour barrier, poor moisture barrier | Thermoformable webs, retort pouches |
| PS | 1.04–1.06 | Clear, rigid, poor barrier | Trays, cups, EPS insulation |
| PVC | 1.30–1.45 | Clear, good fat resistance, variable barrier | Stretch wrap, bottles, trays |
| PVdC | 1.60–1.70 | Excellent gas and moisture barrier | Coatings on OPP and PET |
| EVOH | 1.14–1.19 | Outstanding O₂ barrier (when dry), moisture sensitive | Coextruded barrier layer |
| Ionomer (Surlyn) | 0.94 | Excellent hot tack, seals through contamination | Seal layers, aseptic packaging |
| EVA | 0.93–0.95 | Low seal temperature, flexible, good adhesion | Seal layers, hot melt adhesives |

### 4.4 Paper & Paperboard
- Grammage expressed as g/m². Paperboard defined as >200 g/m² by ISO.
- Inherently permeable to gases, moisture, fats. Requires coating/lamination for barrier.
- Coating with PE, PP, PET, EVOH, aluminium foil, or wax provides barrier and heat sealability.

## 5. Film Coating & Barrier Enhancement

### 5.1 Coating Types
- **Acrylic coatings**: heat sealable, applied to OPP. Seal initiation ~110°C. Coating weight ~1–3 g/m².
- **PVdC coatings**: high O₂ barrier (~10 ml/m²/24h on PET), applied from aqueous dispersion or solvent. Typical weight ~3 g/m² (~2 μm). Specific gravity 1.3.
- **PVOH coatings**: excellent O₂ barrier but moisture sensitive (loses barrier above 65% RH). Must be protected in laminate structure.
- **Low-temperature sealing coatings (LTSC)**: ionomer-based, seal at lower temperatures for faster machine speeds.
- **Metallisation**: vacuum deposition of aluminium on PET, PA, OPP. Dramatically improves barrier. 12 μm PET preferred (large reel area per unit).
- **SiOx coatings**: transparent, retortable, recyclable. Applied by vacuum deposition. 20× O₂ barrier improvement on PET bottles.

### 5.2 Solvent-Based vs Water-Based Coatings
- Industry trend: moving from organic solvent-based to water-based and 100% solids systems to reduce solvent emissions.
- Where solvent-based systems are still used, they are mostly alcohol-based (butanol being the highest boiling point solvent used).
- Coating weight accuracy achieved via etched gravure rollers, up to ~6 g/m².

## 6. Lamination — Adhesive & Extrusion

### 6.1 Adhesive Lamination
- Combines two or more films using adhesive (water-based, solvent-based, or 100% solids).
- **Solvent-based polyurethane adhesives**: applied from gravure roller at 1–3 g/m². Cross-linking provides permanent bond (several hundred g/25mm peel strength).
- CO₂ can form as bubbles during cure — impairs visual quality.
- Adhesive can react with film coatings causing discolouration.
- **100% solids adhesives**: cross-link via heat, UV, or electron beam. No solvent emissions.
- **Pressure to eliminate organic solvents**: regulatory and environmental drivers pushing toward water-based and solventless systems.

### 6.2 Extrusion Lamination
- Molten PE curtain (typically 7–10 g/m²) bonds two webs. Acts as both adhesive and structural layer.
- Surface priming often needed for bond strength >200 g/25mm.
- Minimum practical bond ~100 g/25mm, but specification set higher to account for degradation over time.

### 6.3 Thermal Lamination
- Two heat-sealable webs joined through heated nip rollers. No adhesive added.
- Risk of shrinkage/creasing if temperature too high. Not common for food packaging.

### 6.4 Key Laminate Structures
| Application | Typical Structure | Purpose |
|-------------|-------------------|---------|
| Snack foods (short shelf life) | PVdC-coated OPP / LDPE | O₂ barrier, moisture barrier, seal |
| Snack foods (long shelf life) | Met PET / LDPE | High O₂ barrier, light barrier |
| Retort pouch | PET / Al foil / PP | Heat resistance, absolute barrier, retortable seal |
| Aseptic liquid carton | PE / paperboard / PE / Al foil / PE / EVOH / PE | Structural, barrier, seal |
| Vacuum-packed cheese/meat | PA / PE (thermoformable) | Gas barrier, seal, deep draw |

## 7. Printing on Flexible Packaging

### 7.1 Gravure
- Engraved cylinder with cells holding ink. High initial cost but excellent for long runs at high speed.
- Ink dried in heated oven between each colour station — **this is where solvent drying calculations apply**.
- Solvent-based inks require efficient oven drying to remove solvents before lamination.

### 7.2 Flexographic
- Photopolymer plates on central impression drum. Lower cost for short runs.
- Quality approaching gravure with modern plates and anilox rolls.

### 7.3 Solvent Retention Concerns
- Residual solvents from printing inks and laminating adhesives must be minimised.
- Regulatory limits on residual solvents in food packaging (typically measured in mg/m²).
- Insufficient drying leads to solvent retention → odour/taint in food, delamination risk, regulatory non-compliance.

## 8. Heat Sealing

### 8.1 Methods
- **Hot bar sealing**: most common. Opposing heated jaws apply heat + pressure for set dwell time.
- **Impulse sealing**: brief current pulse heats wire element. Good for thin films.
- **Hot wheel/band sealing**: continuous motion for high-speed lines.
- **Ultrasonic sealing**: mechanical vibration generates heat at interface. No external heat source.
- **Induction sealing**: electromagnetic field heats aluminium foil layer to activate sealant.

### 8.2 Critical Parameters
- **Temperature**: must exceed seal initiation temperature of sealant layer but not damage substrate.
- **Pressure**: ensures intimate contact between seal surfaces.
- **Dwell time**: sufficient for polymer flow and bond formation.
- Insufficient temperature/time → weak seals (channel leaks). Excessive → thinning adjacent to seal, burn-through.
- Seal contamination (product, moisture, dust) is a major cause of defects. Ionomers (Surlyn) seal through contamination.

### 8.3 Seal Testing
- Peel strength: measured on 13–25 mm strips (g/25mm or N/15mm).
- Burst testing: internal pressure resistance (e.g. 280 kPa for 30s for retort pouches).
- Leak detection: vacuum decay, dye penetration, ultrasonic methods.

## 9. Active Packaging

- **Oxygen scavengers**: iron-based sachets or polymer-incorporated systems. Reduce headspace O₂ to <0.01%.
- **CO₂ scavengers/emitters**: control atmosphere in MAP packs.
- **Ethylene scavengers**: activated carbon, potassium permanganate. Extend produce shelf life.
- **Moisture absorbers**: silica gel, molecular sieves, superabsorbent polymers.
- **Antimicrobial packaging**: preservative-releasing or non-migrating contact-active systems.

## 10. Modified Atmosphere Packaging — Technical Detail

### 10.1 Gas Properties
| Gas | Role | Key Effect |
|-----|------|------------|
| CO₂ | Bacteriostatic | Inhibits aerobic bacteria at >20%. Dissolves in product → pack collapse risk |
| N₂ | Inert filler | Prevents pack collapse, displaces O₂ |
| O₂ | Colour maintenance | Maintains oxymyoglobin (red meat) at 70–80%. Inhibits anaerobic pathogens |

### 10.2 Recommended Gas Mixes
| Product | CO₂ (%) | O₂ (%) | N₂ (%) | Shelf Life Extension |
|---------|---------|--------|--------|---------------------|
| Raw red meat | 20–30 | 70–80 | 0 | 2–4 days → 5–8 days |
| Raw poultry | 25–35 | 0 | 65–75 | Significant extension |
| Cooked meats | 25–50 | 0 | 50–75 | Weeks |
| White fish | 40 | 30 | 30 | Days → weeks |
| Oily/smoked fish | 40 | 0 | 60 | Days → weeks |
| Hard cheese | High CO₂ | 0 | Balance | Weeks → months |
| Fresh produce | 2–5 | 2–5 | Balance | Variable by species |

### 10.3 Packaging Film Selection for MAP
- Gas barrier: measured as OTR (O₂ transmission rate) and CO₂TR.
- WVTR: water vapour transmission rate.
- High barrier: PVdC-coated films, metallised PET, EVOH laminates.
- Moderate barrier: uncoated PA, PET.
- Low barrier (for produce): PE, PP, PS — allow gas exchange.
- Antifogging properties needed for chilled products to maintain visibility.
- Mechanical properties must withstand thermoforming, sealing, and distribution.

### 10.4 Quality Assurance
- Seal integrity is a critical control point. Monitor temperature, pressure, dwell time continuously.
- Headspace gas analysis: O₂ and CO₂ measured by dedicated analysers (response time ~2s).
- WVTR measurement: ASTM F1249 (modulated infrared sensor). High-barrier films measurable within 2 days.
- OTR measurement: coulometric sensor (MOCON Oxtran), parts-per-billion sensitivity.

## 11. Retort Pouch — Integrated System

### 11.1 Structure
- Typical: PET (12 μm) / Al foil (9–12 μm) / PP (70–100 μm).
- PET: heat resistance, printability, mechanical strength.
- Al foil: absolute gas/moisture/light barrier.
- PP: heat sealable, retort resistant (up to 135°C).

### 11.2 Production
- Pouches formed from laminate, sealed on 3 sides, filled, evacuated (steam flush or vacuum), closure sealed.
- Production rates: 120–150 pouches/min on modern lines.
- Seal conditions: hot bar or impulse. Temperature, jaw pressure, and dwell time must be monitored.
- Seal contamination must be prevented — grippers stretch pouch opening to prevent wrinkles.

### 11.3 Processing
- Steam-heated water with compressed air overpressure, or steam/air mixtures.
- Overpressure counterbalances internal pressure buildup during heating/cooling transition.
- Pouch thickness positively controlled by specially designed trays (40% void area for heating medium circulation).
- A 2 mm change in thickness → 1.5 min change in F₀ value.

### 11.4 Shelf Life
- Foil-bearing pouches: >2 years at 20°C. Some tested safe at 7 years.
- Foil-free laminates: 4 weeks to 6 months depending on OTR and product sensitivity.

## 12. Environmental & Waste Management

- Lightweighting: yoghurt pot from 11.8g (1970) to 5.0g (1990). Stretch wrap from 1400g to 350g per pallet.
- Recycling: mechanical (sorting, reprocessing), feedstock (pyrolysis, glycolysis back to monomers), energy recovery (incineration with energy capture).
- Biodegradable plastics: PLA (polylactide) from corn/sugar, DuPont Biomax (hydro-biodegradable polyester). Niche applications.
- LCA (Life Cycle Assessment): ISO-standardised methodology. Flexible packaging generally more energy-efficient than rigid alternatives due to lower material weight and transport efficiency.

## 13. Relevance to Lamination Speed Calculations

### What This Book Confirms About Our Calculator Model:

1. **Substrate densities are correct**: PET at ~1.4 g/cm³ and aluminium foil at 2.7 g/cm³ align with the book's material data. The GSM = thickness × density formula is standard industry practice.

2. **Adhesive coat weights are realistic**: The book states polyurethane laminating adhesives are applied at 1–3 g/m² from gravure rollers. Our default of 2.5 g/m² is within this range.

3. **Solvent drying is critical**: The book repeatedly emphasises that residual solvents from printing inks and laminating adhesives must be minimised. Our residual target parameter (mg/m²) directly addresses this regulatory and quality requirement.

4. **Solvent-based to solventless transition**: The industry is moving toward water-based and 100% solids adhesive systems. Our calculator models solvent-based systems which remain widely used, especially in high-performance barrier laminates.

5. **Oven drying between print/lamination stations**: Gravure printing explicitly uses heated ovens between colour stations to dry solvent-based inks. The segmented oven zone model in our calculator mirrors this real-world configuration.

### What Could Be Enhanced (But Does NOT Require Changing Current Logic):

1. **No calculation changes needed**: The core physics model (impingement mass transfer, solvent mass balance, residence time) is sound and not contradicted by anything in this book.

2. **Potential future additions** (not errors in current model):
   - A substrate library could be expanded: PA/Nylon (~1.15 g/cm³), PP (~0.90 g/cm³), LDPE (~0.92 g/cm³), PVdC (~1.6 g/cm³) are all used in food packaging laminates.
   - Water-based adhesive drying could be modelled separately (different latent heat, different evaporation physics).
   - Ink solvent load could be separated from adhesive solvent load for more precise modelling.
   - Barrier property calculations (OTR, WVTR) could complement the speed calculator to help users select appropriate structures.
